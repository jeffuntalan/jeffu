Application of Microbial Identification Techniques in the Species Determination of an Unknown Bacterium

Scientific Paper, Undergraduate Paper
jeffuntalan
August 16, 2021

Jefferson A. Untalan1

1Institute of Biological Sciences, College of Arts and Sciences, University of the Philippines Los Baños, College, Laguna

The identification of bacteria differs greatly than identifying the species of an animal. The microscopic nature of bacteria requires the use of microscopes and various tests to somehow make the invisible, visible. In this series of experiments, an unknown bacterium was identified using gram staining, visual analysis and various metabolic tests. The organism was identified using the inspection of cultural characteristics, morphological features as well as tests that determine the presence of certain enzymes, the organism’s ability to degrade various organic and inorganic molecules. Upon initial inspection, the organism had a cream-colored colony on Nutrient Agar plates. Individual colonies were circular and convex while the margin was entire. The unknown organism was determined to be a non-motile coccus that formed grape-like clusters. Gram staining on the organism showed that it was gram positive. There was no observed capsule during Duguid’s, Maneval’s and Anthony’s methods. The organism was negative for the oxidase test, positive for the catalase test and was observed to be facultative anaerobic. The organism was determined to be fermentative in the Hugh and Liefson test, methyl red positive and positive for VP (Vogues Proskauer) and positive for gelatin hydrolysis. Growth factors also seemed to be a factor in its growth, as it grew well with growth factors. Through all the tests employed on the organism, the results of the tests as well as visual inspection of pointed its probable identity to Staphylococcus aureus, a gram positive coccus that causes a wide range of diseases from minor infections to lethal ones such as pneumonia.

Keywords: microbiology, identification, bacteria

INTRODUCTION

            The need to identify a microorganism stems from the impact it has on the environment or on human life. Knowing the specific properties of a microorganism allows for its cultivation, control and study.

            One of the most important of tests in identification is gram staining. The gram staining method was developed by Hans Christian Joachim Gram, when he was working in a Berlin morgue for the isolation of bacterial pneumonia. He noticed something that would be the cornerstone of future microbial identification techniques. Gram noted that some stains are taken up by the bacteria, while other bacteria do not. While his methods were admittedly crude and imperfect in initial form, years of study improved his technique into the form that we use today. Nowadays however, microbial identification is not solely dependent on how it will react to certain tests. The advent of Polymerase Chain Reaction (PCR) opened the doors for gene-based identification methods through its ability to amplify the otherwise miniscule amount of DNA available from bacteria. However, genetic approaches will not be covered in this study. The focus is instead on the tests and analysis involved in identifying bacteria through its morphology, and metabolism.

            The unknown bacteria focused on in the experiment was assigned in accordance to the course requirement prescribed for the MCB 101 (Microbial Techniques) course. The organism was exposed to a set series of tests starting from morphological tests such as the gram stain, capsule staining methods and flagellar staining methods to metabolism-related tests such as the urease test, oxidase test and catalase test. It was noted that the sensitivity of some tests can be disrupted by some media. The test for acetoin for example, can have different results if the media used is O’Meara’s Fumarate Broth (Steel, 1965). Fortunately, no discrepancies in media and reagents used were present in any of the tests done.

Materials and Methods

            Observations are primarily done in 1000x magnification achieved by using an Oil Immersion Objective.

Gram staining and Gregersen’s method

            The gram staining procedure was done on 16-22h old cultures of the unknown streaked on NA plates.

            Gregersen’s method involved the use of 3% freshly prepared KOH drops on a glass slide. A loopful of the unknown was mixed onto the drops of KOH for about 5 minutes and observed for any signs of viscosity.

Capsule staining

            Capsule staining was done on unknown cultures grown on NAG (NA+2% glucose) and NAS (NA+2% sucrose) slants. Heat fixation is not done in capsule staining techniques to preserve any capsule that may have formed. Duguid’s method (negative staining), Maneval’s method, and Anthony’s method of capsule staining was done in each of the NAG and NAS samples.

Flagellar staining

             The unknown bacteria were inoculated in NA and stored at 5 degrees centigrade below optimal for 24-48 hours to promote flagellar growth. The growth on the NA was then observed for motility using the hanging drop technique.

            The observation of a motility band was also done using Motility medium (MM). A drop of the bacterial suspension from the previous NA slant was dropped on the surface of MM, a stab was also done on another MM. The media was then inoculated for two days and observed for the presence of a motility band.

            Modified Bailey’s method was used on the unknown bacterial samples. The slides were new, and were cleaned thoroughly to prevent the destruction of the flagella.

Granular staining

            The unknown was grown on N-limited Glucose Yeast Extract Agar (GYEA) for this part and was incubated for 24-48 hours.

            Intracellular lipids were stained by heat fixing the unknown and flooding it with Sudan black. The excess was rinsed off and xylene was used to rinse the slide once more. Safranin was then added for about 10 seconds and the slide was washed with water.

            Staining for starch and glycogen granules involved the use of Lugol’s iodine mixed into the unknown on a clean glass slide.

            Metachromatic granules were observed by dropping methylene blue on the heat-fixed unknown samples.

Endospore staining

            For endospore staining, the unknown bacteria were grown on an NA slant and an NA plate while Germination medium (GM) was prepared beforehand.

            Staining of the endospores using the Shaeffer-Fulton method involved two separate methods: a steam method, and a cold method.

            The germination of endospores was done by overlaying 10mL GM onto an unknown bacteria NA slant and dislodging the growing bacteria. The suspension was put into a drum vial and an additional 10mL of GM was added in. Serial dilution was done and the resulting diluted samples were pour plated onto NA plates and counted after growth.

Oxygen Requirement

            The determination of oxygen requirement needed unknowns grown in Fluid Thioglycollate Medium and NA slants.

            The unknown bacteria were inoculated onto recently FTM while the medium was still molten at around 45 degrees Celsius. The bacteria were stabbed onto the media using a wire needle and mixed gently, avoiding aeration of the medium.

            The unknowns grown on the NA slant were used on the catalase test, and the oxidase test.

Growth factor and temperature requirements

            The unknown bacterial samples were grown in Growth Factor Test Media A and B and observed for growth.

            The unknown was also grown in NA and incubated at varying temperatures at 10 degrees, 30 degrees, 45 degrees, and 60 degrees Celsius for 2-5 days and observed.

Metabolism of carbohydrates and organic acids

            O/F test, Methyl Red test, Vogues-Proskauer test was done on the unknown samples. Citric acid utilization on Simmon’s Citrate Agar, Malonate utilization, starch hydrolysis, and the production of 2-ketogluconate were also tested.

Decomposition of large molecules

            The tests on decomposition of large molecules is done in three parts. The first test is for the decomposition of gelatin, where the unknowns are inoculated onto nutrient gelatin and observed for the solidification of gelatin after incubation of about 48-72 hours.

            Lipolytic activity is tested using Sierra’s medium with Tween80 (ST). The unknown is streaked onto ST and incubated for 48-72 hours.

            Litmus milk (LM) is used for testing the degradation of lactose. The unknown is inoculated onto LM tubes, incubated at 33 degrees for 24-48 hours.

Nitrogen and sulfur metabolism

            The unknown bacteria were tested for the utilization of organic and inorganic nitrogen by streaking it onto Nitrogen Media A and B (NMA and NMB) Growth was then observed and noted after incubation.

            Urea hydrolysis was tested by inoculating the unknown heavily onto Urea broth until turbid. The broths were then incubated for 4-6 hours and observed.

            Indole test was performed by adding 2 drops of Kovac’s reagent onto unknowns grown on Tryptone broth (TB).

            Phenylalanine decarboxylation was tested on the unknown by inoculating it onto Lysine decarboxylase broth (LDB) and observing for changes in color.

            Nitrate reduction was test by inoculating the unknown onto Nutrient broth+0.1%KNO3 with a Durham tube. And observing for signs of gas production after incubation. Addition of nitrate reagent A and B was also done.

            Hydrogen Sulfide production was also tested by stabbing the Lead Acetate Agar (LAA) and streaking the surface of the same slant. Observation followed after incubation at 30 degrees Celsius for 48 hours.

The results of the tests were compared to existing records. The first characteristic of the unknown is its coccus shape and its gram positive property. The shape alone narrows the species down very well since the clustering of the cocci comes next into their identification. Since the observed clusters of cells were grape-like, other species were ruled out temporarily. Chain-like clusters are linked to Streptococcus, pairs are associated with Neisseria and tetrads are associated with Micrococcus (Adams & Moss, 2008). The suspected identity of the unknown based on visual analysis, leads to Staphylococcus.

            Other notable characteristics is its non-motility, given than it has no flagella. The cells are also non-capsulated and has no noticeable endospore.

            The metabolic tests can be used to further descend down the hierarchy of taxonomy. Among the species of the Staphylococci genus, Staphylococcus epidermis was ruled out because it cannot hydrolyze gelatin while the unknown bacteria can. The results greatly point to the identity of the unknown to be Staphylococcus aureus.

Terminal Restriction Fragment Length Polymorphism (TRFLP): A Culture-Independent Method of Microbial Identification

Scientific Paper, Undergraduate Paper
jeffuntalan
August 15, 2021

Literature Review

Terminal Restriction Fragment Length Polymorphism (TRFLP or T-RFLP) analysis is a culture-independent method (CIM) of microbial identification commonly employed in areas with complex microbial communities such as soil, activated sludge systems, and even in inside the human body. Culture-independent techniques are particularly useful in cases where the desired bacterial species are unculturable or very difficult to grow in the laboratory such as the bacterial samples present in soil samples and about 50% of  bacteria that are found in the human colon (Ream et al., 2013). TRFLP is PCR-based, and it utilizes variations on the 16S rRNA to “fingerprint” entire microbial communities within their environments, allowing for non-culturable species to be profiled (Su et al., 2012).  

The TRFLP assay can be outlined in four major steps: (1) sample collection and DNA extraction, (2) PCR amplification with fluorescent primers and addition of restriction enzymes, (3) separation and detection of digested products, (4) data analysis, and clustering analysis of the microbial profiles from previously analyzed data. An in-depth explanation of each step will be provided in the next section.

The TRFLP Procedure

The principle of TRFLP lies in the primers used during the (PCR) amplification stage. A pair of primers are used, and one of them is fluorescently labeled.  The amplicons are then digested with restriction enzymes that yields terminal restriction fragments (TRFs) with fluorescent labels. The TRFs are denatured, and their single strand DNAs are separated using and electrophoresis platform. The fluorescent primers allow for the laser detection of the TRFs and its estimation via automated data analysis software such as GeneMapper (T-RFLP – BioMineWiki, n.d.). Sample Collection, DNA Extraction

The first step in the TRFLP assay is the collection of samples. The environment where bacteria of interest grow will give a general idea of what known species are present, their properties, and in what conditions unknown species living in the same area might grow in. The property of the sample is also indicative of what DNA extraction method to use. Soil samples, for example, can be extracted using direct lysis while probiotics can be extracted using the phenol-chloroform method (Tien et al., 1999). Although TRFLP is useful for its culture-independence, it falls prey to certain representational biases brought by DNA extraction. DNA extraction methods that work with one species may not work well with another and as a result, some bacterial communities may get overrepresented while others may be underrepresented or even completely overlooked. Culture-based approaches also fall prey to this bias to a certain degree, since only bacterial samples whose growing conditions are understood can be cultured. However, culture-based approaches can provide very sensitive detection since single cells can be distinguished. In ecological studies however, CIMs are better alternatives to culture-based approaches (Ream et al., 2013).

PCR Amplification with fluorescent primers, and addition of restriction enzymes

After the DNA extraction of the desired population of bacteria, the next step would be to amplify the targeted genes via PCR. A pair of Oligonucleotide primers, which can be scouted for using services such as BLAST and FunGene, is used to flank the desired region of DNA to be replicated. One of the primers is labelled at the 5’ terminus end with a fluorescent dye to facilitate easier reading (Osborne, 2014). One drawback of utilizing PCR is its inherent bias to amplify already dominant groups in the population. This is because dominant groups tend to have a higher chance of pickup up the primers and getting amplified (Stres, 2006). However, according to Kent and Triplett (2002) the presence or absence of a fragment in the reading is not a determinant of the presence or absence of a population from a sample, but is instead  interpreted as degree of detection of the fragment.

The PCR conditions and primer selection can be optimized depending on the projected populations that may be present in the area of sample collection to further increase yield.

After PCR, the removal of any unincorporated primers, dNTPs, and buffer must be done to purify the amplicons prior to the addition of endonuclease restriction enzymes. The amplicons will then be digested with restriction endonucleases to reduce them to the desired length. This will yield fragments that do not have the labeled primer, which will not be detected (Ream et al., 2013). Electrophoresis in the next step will confirm that the amplicons now have the desired length.

Separation of digested products and detection using electrophoresis.

The amplicon mixture at this stage is composed of labeled and unlabeled fragments which can be separated via capillary electrophoresis (CE) or PAGE. During electrophoresis, fragments without the fluorescent tag can still be visualized as bars as in the case of gel electrophoresis. The resulting data can be read by a fluorescence detector as a series of peaks.

Capillary electrophoresis is more commonly used than gel electrophoresis (Ercolini & Cocolin, 2014). CE is preferred due to its higher sensitivity and resolution, but this comes at a cost: CE requires and expensive machine and matching software to properly utilize it. In resource-scarce areas however, gel electrophoresis will suffice (Gupta et al., 2010).

Data Analysis

Only fragments with a fluorescent tag will be picked up by a fluorescence detector and any untagged fragments will be ignored. The resulting data is a series of peaks that denote the detected fragments which can be interpreted as individual species from the study site. In the early days of TRFLP, much of data analysis relied on visual inspection of peaks. Samples with a large amount of noise (negligible peaks that may appear in the data) can be tricky to analyze and the misjudgment of which can lead to further difficulties in analysis (Prakash et al., 2014). Further steps in refining the TRFLP data can be done through the assignment of a peak detection threshold (PDT), alignment of fragments, and applying normalization procedures to the data.

A peak detection threshold works like a noise filter, since it ignores value that fall below the threshold and allows values that exceed it to manifest. However, care must be observed when placing this value as a too low value may cause the overestimation of diversity in the sample while a high threshold may result in an underestimation of diversity and even the exclusion of trace species of microorganisms.

Alignment refers to the grouping of similar or closely sized fragments. The alignment of fragments is done to provide a baseline of similarity between two or more fragment profiles which normally comes from replicates of the sample area. Fragment sizes are normally decimals and are converted into integers at the first stage of alignment. They are then “binned” into the integers they are closest to by a pre-defined value.

Normalization, sometimes referred to as standardization, is done to remove any differences that in the fragment profiles which stems from the differences of the number of nucleic acids that were loaded into the gel. This is an important step as variances in the numbers of DNA/RNA can be hard to avoid when working with very small volumes. Normalization can be done by assessing the height and width of the peaks that are generated when the fluorescent primers attached to the fragments are scanned and digitized.

Analysis of data can be done through software such as Applied Biosystems’ GeneMapper or GeneScan. Online tools are also present, such as T-RFLP Expedited (T-REX) which is free, only requires access to the website and will soon be open source (Culman et al., 2009). Software such as these help to filter out the noise and the alignment of peaks which have the same length.

Weaknesses of TRFLP

TRFLP, as previously mentioned, falls prey to biases involving DNA extraction and PCR. These biases affect similar methods that rely on DNA extraction and PCR and is not solely found in TRFLP. The choice of restriction enzymes also imparts bias to the TRFLP analysis. An unoptimized restriction enzyme can provide a high variability of species and diversity to samples which can lead to a misrepresentation of dominant species in an area (Ghosh & Bhadury, 2018).

While the method of TRFLP is well-established, there is still no universal way to analyze data. Choices in data analysis affect TRLFP data can have a big effect on the accuracy of the output. TRFLP data is read is interpreted through a series of peaks which indicate the present species but relying on peaks alone can be unfavorable for lengthy fragments because long fragments travel for longer times in electrophoresis due to their higher molecular weight. This is observed as low and wide peaks in the data which can be underestimated if species abundance is based on peak height. Using peak widths can also be considered when analyzing data, but is has been shown that species abundance can also be influenced when using peak widths if the peaks overlap with one another due to their low and wide profiles (Fredriksson et al., 2014).

Reproducibility can be improved, however, by employing certain novel methods. Fredriksson et al. (2014) has shown that increasing the detection threshold (PDT) paired with preferring peak heights during normalization can lead to more consistent results.

Advantages of TRFLP

Despite the slew of factors that may affect TRFLP’s output, it remains a very versatile tool when done correctly. It provides a rapid solution with a very high throughput that is not limited to a single group of organisms (Prakash et al., 2014). It can be used for any microorganism that contains sufficient genetic material to be tagged, from a wide range of environments.

In terms of cost, TRFLP requires a minimal amount of resources as it requires apparatuses that are not specialized and ubiquitous in most molecular biology labs. In terms of software, free tools can be found online aside from other paid options. This allows TRFLP to be conducted by on a relatively low budget.
